Ruthenium

Ruthenium is a chemical element with symbol Ru and atomic number 44. Classified as a transition metal, Ruthenium is a solid at room temperature.

Most is used in the electronics industry for chip resistors and electrical contacts. Ruthenium oxide is used in the chemical industry to coat the anodes of electrochemical cells for chlorine production. Ruthenium is also used in catalysts for ammonia and acetic acid production.

Physical properties:

  • Ruthenium is a hard, brittle metal with a silvery-white appearance.
  • It has a high melting point and boiling point, making it resistant to heat and deformation.
  • It is a good conductor of electricity and heat.

Chemical properties:

Occurrence:

  • Ruthenium is a relatively rare element, found in small amounts in certain minerals and ores.
  • It is typically obtained as a byproduct of mining other platinum group metals.

History.

The story of ruthenium's discovery unfolds in the early 19th century. While clues emerged earlier, suggesting the presence of an unknown element, definitive confirmation arrived in 1844.

Early indications of ruthenium came from studies of platinum ores. In 1807, a Polish chemist observed potential traces of a new element, but later retracted his claim due to difficulties in replicating the results. Further investigations by other scientists yielded similar tantalizing hints.

Finally, in 1844, Russian chemist Karl Klaus successfully isolated and characterized ruthenium. He named it after 'Ruthenia,' an old name for Russia, honoring the origins of the sample he used. This marked a significant step in expanding the elemental map and understanding the properties of this unique metal.

Usage.

  • Electrical components: Ruthenium's ability to withstand high temperatures and resist corrosion makes it valuable for electrical contacts and switches in demanding environments.
  • Chemical catalysts: Ruthenium acts as a catalyst in various chemical reactions, accelerating processes in industries like petroleum refining and chemical manufacturing.
  • Durable alloys: When added to other metals, ruthenium enhances their strength and resistance to wear and tear. These high-temperature alloys find use in jet engines, rocket components, and other demanding applications.
Some of the benefits of using nuthenium are:
  • Ruthenium-alloyed steel can be used for stronger and more durable bridges, buildings, and pipelines.
  • Ruthenium-containing components in jet engines and rockets contribute to safer and more efficient air travel.
  • Ruthenium's role in catalysts can lead to more efficient chemical processes, potentially benefiting various industries.

Sources.

  • Ruthenium occurs naturally in association with other platinum group metals (PGMs) in certain minerals. These minerals are typically found in specific geological formations and require specialized mining and processing techniques to extract the desired elements, including ruthenium.
  • In some cases, ruthenium can be recovered as a byproduct during the processing of nickel and copper ores. This is because these ores may contain trace amounts of platinum group metals, including ruthenium.
